The Brief

An E-Commerce Brand Losing Customers at Checkout

A fictional e-commerce brand has a cluttered layout causing users to drop off before completing a purchase. The task is to design a clean, logical homepage structure that guides users toward conversion through hierarchy alone — no colour, no imagery, no decoration.

The Problem

Existing layout is cluttered and creates high cognitive load. Users are abandoning before reaching checkout because friction accumulates across every section of the page.

High cognitive load No clear hierarchy Weak CTA placement

The Approach

Design the skeleton of a homepage for TREAD — a minimalist everyday sneaker brand — using Z-pattern layout, intentional whitespace, and three strategically placed CTAs at key scroll depths.

Key Design Decisions

What Was Decided Before a Single Frame Was Drawn

Every structural decision in this wireframe traces back to a specific brief requirement or psychological principle. Nothing was placed by feel.

01

Z-Pattern over F-Pattern

The brief recommends Z-pattern for single conversion goal pages. TREAD's homepage has one primary goal — get the user to a product. Logo top-left, nav top-right, hero diagonal, CTA bottom-left of hero.

02

Single Hero CTA

Original draft had two CTAs above the fold — "Shop Men" and "Shop Women." This splits attention before the user has engaged with the brand. Consolidated to one: "Shop Now."

03

Three Category Tiles, Not Four

Removed "Best Sellers" from category tiles — it duplicated the dedicated Best Sellers product section directly below. Fewer options, cleaner decision point.

04

Three Product Cards, Not Four

The brief's whitespace slide shows three cards as the focused version. Three breathes and feels curated. Four crowds without adding meaningful choice.

05

Section Order as Narrative

Discover → Browse → Go deep on one product → Understand the brand → Trust signals → Capture. Each section earns its place by moving the user one step closer to a decision.

06

Annotations on Every Section

The brief explicitly frames annotations as connective tissue between design intent and developer execution. Every section has an inline annotation explaining its purpose in the user journey.

Annotation Summary

What Each Section Does and Why It's There

Annotations explain intent, not appearance. Each one answers: what is this, what does it do, and why does it belong at this position in the user journey.

01

Announcement Bar

Surfaces key trust and shipping information immediately — free shipping threshold and return policy — before the user has scrolled at all.

02

Navigation

Simplified to four links — Men, Women, New Arrivals, Sale — reducing cognitive load and helping users reach categories quickly without decision fatigue.

03

Hero Section

Introduces the brand and drives one clear first action with a single primary CTA. Z-pattern positions the CTA at the diagonal end-point where the eye naturally lands.

04

Category Tiles

Three visual entry points into the catalogue — Men, Women, New Arrivals — without overwhelming users with too many options. Visual blocks, not text links.

05

Best Sellers

Surfaces the three most popular products early to shorten the path from homepage to product exploration. Three cards with breathing room rather than a dense grid.

06

Meet Tread One

Highlights the flagship product in more detail to create a strong product-led conversion moment before the user reaches trust-building content.

07

Why Tread

Reinforces the brand's core product benefits — comfort, lightweight design — to build confidence and drive purchase decisions at the mid-scroll hesitation point.

08

Trust Strip

Addresses transactional concerns — free shipping, returns, secure checkout, support — at the point where a user is close to deciding but hasn't committed yet.

09

Email Signup

Captures interest from users who aren't ready to buy now with a clear incentive — 10% off first order. Converts browsers into a retargetable audience.

10

Footer

Provides essential links and support information without distracting from the primary journey. Four columns: Shop, Help, About, Follow Us.

Core Principle

Structure Before Aesthetics

Design Principle

If a stranger cannot identify what you sell and how to buy it within 5 seconds, friction is too high.

The 5-second test from the brief guided every structural decision in this wireframe. TREAD's homepage passes it: the hero headline, product image placeholder, and single CTA make the offer immediately legible before the user has read a single word of body copy.

Respect the Brain

Every unnecessary option, duplicated section, or competing CTA was removed. Cognitive load reduction is not a nice-to-have — it is the conversion strategy.

Guide the Eye

Z-pattern layout and active whitespace direct attention without the user noticing. Every section transition has a logical reason to scroll further.

Earn Every Section

Each of the ten sections exists because it moves the user one step closer to a decision. No section was included because homepages usually have it.
